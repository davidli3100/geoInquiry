Scientists and International Consortiums Have Been Working Towards the Goal Of Achieving an Efficient Catalyst and Process To Convert Emissions To Useful Products

MIT - Researchers at MIT have found a new way of converting Carbon Dioxide Emissions (CO2) into Carbon Monoxide (CO).


Carbon Monoxide is the base and first step in converting emissions into usable green fuels, like hydrogen or ethanol. Using an advanced version of previous catalysts, scientists were able to expand their pores and increase CO output efficiency. 

Using renewable sources of electricity, the researchers create a process designed to replace and supplement renewable sources, when interupted. (Such as replacing solar panels when they can't produce energy during the night)

This is an example of the catalyst and process that occurs when converting CO2 to CO. Source: http://energy.mit.edu/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/160926_2113.jpg

This entire process has become much more efficient in producing products with Hydrogen and CO in them, rather than more Carbon being released. CO can be converted into many different liquid fuels, such as ethanol, shown below.

Using the MIT Researcher's extremely efficient process of converting CO2 to CO, researchers around the world can now mark this problem as solved and proceed to the next big step; converting CO to liquid fuels. 

STANFORD UNIVERSITY

Piggybacking off of MIT's CO2 Catalyst Research, Scientists Have Discovered a New Way To Create Ethanol In an Environmentally Sustainable Fashion.

 The Nanocrystalline Copper Catalyst Used To Convert Carbon Monoxide To Ethanol    Source: https://s3-us-west-1.amazonaws.com/stanford.ucomm.newsms.media/wp-  content/uploads/2017/06/16151009/copper_hand_3812-1-555x370.jpg

Stanford University - Professor Thomas Jaramillo and his team at the Stanford University Faculty of Chemical Engineering Science have discovered a new way to convert Carbon Monoxide to Ethanol.

Ethanol is a viable Bio-Fuel and has several use cases in modern day transportation and industry. Sustainable and environmentally friendly production of this fuel is a must-have if we want to stop global warming. 

Jaramillo has pioneered a new method, using copper, to produce ethanol without relying on crops, and by taking in CO2 and CO that would have otherwise gone into the atmosphere.

This technology will address and improve on some fundamental flaws of Bio-Fuels. CO2 emissions will be reduced, rather than increased, and the supply of crops associated with ethanol will not be affected. 


The Science Behind It

To successfully convert Carbon Monixide (CO) to Ethanol fuel, Jaramillo uses Electrochemical CO2 reduction to generate ethanol at high amounts of efficiency never done before.

Ethanol is created by using electrocatalysts that the Stanford team has developed. Using this proprietary method, they improve the Faraday efficiency of the catalyst to 57% and ensure that the energy being expended is being used as appropriately as possible. 

Copper is used throughout this process because it is one of the only catalysts that create ethanol as a product at room temperature.

Producing Ethanol at room temperature greatly saves on energy expenditure since no extra heating or cooling will be required. Adding to the efficiency and "Eco-Friendliness" of this project.

Using the copper electrocatalysts, scientists reduce CO2 and produce hydrocarbons, which will combust and create more CO2 that can be converted to CO.  (Using a process derived from MIT's experiments)

This CO is then converted to ethanol by putting the a copper electrocatalyst fuel cell inside CO satured water and then applying a small voltage across it.

This voltage is sourced from a renewable electricity source, staying in line with the eco-friendly theme. 

After applying the current, the fuel cell reacts with the CO to generate ethanol, with zero emissions produced from this reaction.

The ethanol produced is considered to be free of any environmental impacts, production of the CO that created the ethanol was from CO2 emissions. The electricity used in the reaction, sourced from renewable sources. This process is quite efficient as well, making good use fo the electricity and energy put into it, at a 57% efficiency rate. 

Geographic Impacts

Bio-Fuel is notorious for affecting the food supply, climate, and natural ecosystems of areas where they are farmed. 

Ethanol is usually procured from crops such as corn. Massive swaths of land are clear cut to plant crops solely for the purpose of creating ethanol. Making this process as destructive to the environment as using fossil fuels. 

Entire ecosystems, destroyed. People, displaced from their homes. As of now, ethanol as a bio-fuel isn't an environmentally or socially responsible form of fuel.

However, with the advancement of science, and processes such as this being scaled up to greater levels of production and efficiency, we can hope for a greener future where our environment is preservered and means of production are socially responsible.
